Future perspectives of Halal Tourism

Welcome to Room 4, where we look ahead at the future perspectives of Halal tourism.

Muslims are populating the world at a quick and steady rate. The growth of this population is predicted to reach 25.13% by 2020 and 33.14% by 2075, as Islam is the second-largest religion with steadily increasing adherents (Kettani, 2010). Pew Research estimates that there were 1.6 billion Muslims worldwide in 2010, and that number is projected to rise to 2.8 billion by 2050, accounting for around 30% of the global population (Economist, 2011).

According to Henderson (2016), Muslims are concentrated mainly in the Asia-Pacific area, where they make up over 60% of the population. The remaining 20% live in the Middle East and North Africa, where they account for 93% of the resident population. In order to meet the demands of this growing population, the global market's attention has been drawn to the "Halal" business sector.

Data indicates that global Muslims spent $140 billion on outbound travel in 2013 alone, excluding the Hajj and Umrah. In contrast, total worldwide spending in 2013 was $1,212 billion (Reuters, 2015).

There is growing awareness of Halal Tourism among providers, despite the language obstacles that exist between Muslim tourists and their destinations, and the conflicts between modern global standards and Islamic teachings. Despite these challenges, the rising Muslim population, easier access to travel information, and the numerous locations starting to tailor their services to Muslim needs will be the main factors encouraging growth. In a nutshell, Halal tourism is a trending sector that presents significant commercial prospects for both OIC member countries and non-OIC member countries.
