MODERN Muslim Tourism

Welcome to Room 2, where we explore the current state of tourism among Muslim travelers.

As we said, history of halal tourism dates back to around the 7th century when Islam-familiar merchants started doing business and talking about Islam. Since the very beginning of Islam, Muslims have never stopped showing their genuine interest in traveling, and in recent years, this has evolved into Halal tourism.

Muslim travelers leave their homes in search of fresh horizons, interact with "others," and then return with an enhanced sense of difference and similarity. Even if travelers think they are transcending them, travel actually generates limits and distinctions.

The connection between Islam and tourism has garnered more awareness during the past few years. The term "halal tourism" was first coined in the early 2000s, and the industry has grown rapidly since then. Researchers and the tourism industry are paying more and more attention to ideas like Muslim tourism (Scott and Jafari, 2010), halal tourism (WTM, 2007), Islamic tourism (Battour et al., 2017), Sharia-compliant hotels (Henderson, 2010), halal hospitality, and others. However, they are frequently surrounded by some ambiguity (Hamza et al., 2012).

Jeffery, Chairman of World Travel Market, defined Halal tourism as a type of religious tourism that represents allowable activities under Islamic teachings in terms of behavior, dress, conduct, and diet (Battour et al., 2012). The first halal-friendly hotels and resorts began to appear in Muslim-majority countries in the mid-2000s, and since then, many countries and companies have been actively promoting halal tourism as a way to tap into the growing Muslim travel market.

Women and Halal Tourism
Throughout history, Muslim women’s tourist mobilities have been limited by social, cultural, and religious factors. However, in recent times, Muslim women travelers have emerged as a growing subsector, whether traveling for work, leisure, with friends, or alone. In 2018, the number of Muslim women travelers was estimated at 63 million (45% of all Muslim travelers). Halal tourism helps Muslim women gain more freedom by assuring they can maintain their safety and dignity, making travel without Mahrams acceptable within this framework.

Modern Families and Halal Tourism
Research conducted by the inaugural Asia Family Travel (2012) shows that families are willing to spend more on vacations, especially in Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, the Philippines, and India. 74% of travelers are families with children aged 19 and below. There is a trend toward families seeking adventure and authentic experiences, making it important to understand their specific motives for travel.
